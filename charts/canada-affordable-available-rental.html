What the terms mean

Lowest-income renter households
The poorest 20% of renter (tenant) households in each province/territory - the bottom income quintile. Used here as Canada's stand-in for the U.S. "extremely low-income" group.
Affordable & available
A home a lowest-income renter can occupy for less than 30% of their income. Measured here as the number of lowest-income renters actually housed below that threshold (the Census does not count vacant units).
Shelter-cost-to-income ratio (STIR)
Share of a household's total before-tax income spent on shelter (rent + utilities, etc.). Under 30% is considered affordable; 30% or more means the household is cost-burdened.
Per 100
Of every 100 lowest-income renter households, how many are housed affordably. Lower = deeper shortage.

Published June 2026

Inspiration National Low Income Housing Coalition (NLIHC) - The Gap: A Shortage of Affordable Homes (March 2026), which maps this measure for U.S. states. This is an independent Canadian recreation of that idea; it is not affiliated with or endorsed by NLIHC.
Data source Statistics Canada, Census of Population 2021 - Table 98-10-0252-01, renter households by household total income group × shelter-cost-to-income ratio. Retrieved 19 Jun 2026. DOI: 10.25318/9810025201-eng.
Method The lowest income quintile stands in for NLIHC's "extremely low income"; figures use the 2021 Census (NLIHC uses 2024 data) and exclude vacant units, so they slightly understate available supply. Results were cross-checked against Statistics Canada's separately published renter cost-burden rates.
