Notes

What is measured
Greenhouse gas emissions (in tonnes CO₂e) from burning fossil fuels and wood directly in homes: natural gas, heating oil, propane, coal, and wood. Electricity-related GHG are excluded at the provincial level in the source dataset because provincial grids vary enormously in carbon intensity and are accounted for separately in Canada’s national emissions inventory.
Why Quebec and Newfoundland appear lowest
Both provinces heat predominantly with electricity from hydropower, producing very little on-site combustion. Their low values reflect minimal oil or gas burned inside homes, not necessarily lower energy use overall.
Precision and methodology
Per-household GHG is derived from each province’s CEUD GHG intensity (t/TJ) multiplied by total residential energy use (PJ), then divided by the provincial household count. All three inputs are from the same CEUD provincial Table 1 (2023). For small provinces (NL, PEI, NB), the underlying energy figures carry more model uncertainty than for larger ones.
Territories
Yukon, the Northwest Territories, and Nunavut are not covered by the CEUD provincial tables used here and appear in grey on the map.
Data source Natural Resources Canada, Comprehensive Energy Use Database (CEUD) 2023, Residential Sector, Table 1 (Secondary Energy Use and GHG Emissions by Energy Source) for each province. Retrieved June 2026. oee.nrcan.gc.ca.
Method GHG per household = (GHG intensity × total residential energy use) ÷ provincial household count. All inputs from CEUD provincial Table 1, 2023 edition. Both GHG and household counts are CEUD model-based estimates, derived from the Survey of Household Energy Use and Statistics Canada population data.
